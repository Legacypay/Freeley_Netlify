Medical Education

How do these medications work?

Semaglutide works by mimicking a natural hormone called GLP-1. It works systemically across five core biological zones to regulate blood sugar, slow digestion, and reduce appetite.

Brain
  • • Significantly reduced appetite
  • • Increased feeling of fullness (satiety)
Heart
  • • Reduced risk of heart attack and cardiovascular incidents
Adipose Tissue GIP
  • • Increased insulin sensitivity
  • • Increased fat metabolism and storage capacity
GI Tract
  • • Decreased stomach emptying
  • • Reduced gastrointestinal motility
Pancreas
  • • Regulates glucagon secretion
  • • Optimizes insulin secretion capacity
Liver
  • • Increased baseline insulin sensitivity

Common Questions

Everything you
need to know

The active ingredients (semaglutide, tirzepatide) are FDA-approved. However, compounded medications are not FDA-approved as finished drug products. They are prepared by licensed 503A pharmacies under physician supervision with pharmaceutical-grade ingredients.
Most patients report reduced appetite within the first 1–2 weeks. Meaningful weight loss (5–10%) typically occurs within the first 4–12 weeks, with continued loss over 6–12 months of treatment.
The most common side effects are nausea, constipation, and reduced appetite — often temporary and managed by dose titration. Your physician will supervise your dosing schedule to minimize discomfort.
GLP-1 medications are most effective as subcutaneous (under-skin) injections, done weekly at home with a tiny needle. Many patients say it's nearly painless. Detailed instructions and support are always available.
Some weight regain is possible after stopping, as the medication is metabolically active. We recommend using the treatment period to establish lasting habits, and our physicians can help design a maintenance plan.
Compounded GLP-1 medications are typically not covered by insurance, which is partly why our pricing is far below brand-name alternatives. FSA and HSA cards are accepted.
